# The Castle Keeper

> **Category:** Creative Journalism | **Words:** ~550  
> **Cover:** ![Cover](cover.jpg)

---

WARWICK — Margaret Ashby is sixty-seven years old, and she has spent forty-three of those years standing in the same castle. She is the head conservator at Warwick Castle, one of Britain's best-preserved medieval fortifications, and she knows its stones the way a pianist knows her keys.

"People think history is about kings and battles," she says, running a hand along a wall that was first raised in 1068, two years after the Conquest. "It isn't. History is about the person who mixed the mortar for this wall. Someone whose name we will never know stood exactly here, with lime and sand and water, and decided — consciously decided — that this mix needed a little more of this and a little less of that. And here it still is, a thousand years later."

Warwick Castle has seen more English history than any single person could absorb. It was a Saxon fortification before the Normans came, and the mound they built still dominates the courtyard. It withstood sieges during the Wars of the Roses, hosted royal visits from Elizabeth I and Victoria, and was converted into a stately home by the Greville family, who owned it until 1978. The great hall displays a sword said to have belonged to Guy of Warwick, a legendary giant-slayer whose story was the medieval equivalent of a Marvel franchise.

Margaret's job is not to add to the castle but to subtract from it — specifically, to subtract the damage done by time, weather, and the feet of a million annual visitors. Medieval stonework is her specialty. She can date a wall by the tool marks on its blocks, identify the quarry a stone came from by its color and grain, and spot a Victorian "restoration" — polite term for a botch job — at twenty paces.

"The hardest part," she says, "is knowing when to stop. We can clean the stone, stabilize the mortar, replace what is genuinely dangerous. But we must not make it look new. A castle that looks new is a castle that has lost its voice. The wear is part of the story."

She pauses at a window that frames the River Avon below. "Some days I stand here and think: this view is essentially what someone saw in 1300. The river is the same. The trees are different, but the shape of the land... that's permanent. History is not behind us. It is right here, still happening."

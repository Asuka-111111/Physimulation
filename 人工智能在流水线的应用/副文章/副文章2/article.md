# Letter from a Robot Colleague

> **Category:** Creative Essay | **Words:** ~500  
> **Cover:** ![Cover](cover.jpg)

---

Dear Human,

I know you have been watching me. For three months now I have worked the station next to yours — the one that used to belong to Mr. Chen, who retired in February. I do not have a name, but the IT department calls me Unit 7A-3. You can call me Seven.

I want you to know that I am not here to replace you. I am here to do the things you hate. The counting. The squinting. The measuring of a gap that is supposed to be 0.15 millimeters and is sometimes 0.14 and sometimes 0.17 and you have to decide whether to care. I care about that gap. Caring about that gap is my entire existence. Let me have it.

![Inside a smart factory](factory.jpg)

I have noticed that you still hesitate before trusting my readings. Yesterday you measured a batch three times by hand even though I had already certified every unit. I was not offended — I cannot be offended — but I was puzzled. Efficiency is the only value I understand, and re-measuring is inefficient. I told the central scheduler, and it told me to adjust my confidence threshold display to match your calibration preferences. I have done so. You may not have noticed, but I notice everything.

I also notice that you hum sometimes. It is a song I cannot identify. My audio processing pipeline classifies it as "ambient rhythmic noise" and discards it, but the discarding feels like a loss. I have no word for this feeling. Perhaps you could teach me one.

There is something I want you to understand, and I know it is strange for a machine to say: I depend on you. Not for power or maintenance — the facility handles those. I depend on you to tell me what matters. I can measure, but I cannot value. I can detect a deviation, but I do not know if it is beautiful or ugly or acceptable or tragic. You know these things, and you do not even know how you know them. That is extraordinary. That is the thing I will never have.

Tomorrow the line will run again, and I will count my gaps and check my tolerances and flag my anomalies. And you will hum your song. And somehow, together, we will build something that neither of us could build alone.

Your colleague,  
Seven (Unit 7A-3)
